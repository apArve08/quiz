📖 Chapter Recap & Study Guide

🎓 Key Learning Outcomes

After completing this chapter and quiz, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish Maintenance Types: Differentiate between Corrective (fixing), Adaptive (environment), Perfective (enhancing), and Preventive (future-proofing) tasks.
  • Understand System Decay: Explain Software Aging and how Technical Debt and Architectural Erosion make systems harder to maintain over time.
  • Manage Legacy Systems: Identify the risks of replacing legacy systems and the role of Reengineering vs Refactoring.
  • Apply Metrics: Use MTBF (Reliability), MTTR (Repair speed), and COCOMO (Cost estimation) to quantitatively assess software health.
  • Navigate Evolution Models: Compare the Quick Fix, Iterative Enhancement, and Full Reuse models of software evolution.

📚 Comprehensive Chapter Summaries (Based on Course Notes)

Chapter 1: Software Maintenance & Evolution
Core Concept: Modifying products post-delivery to meet changing needs.
  • Real-World Impact: The 2018 Facebook "View As" bug illustrates how a maintenance modification can inadvertently introduce critical vulnerabilities.
  • Critical Nature: Maintenance is often harder than development because foreseen changes are rare.
  • Common Failures: Issues typically arise from Lack of Traceability (code not linked to requirements), Lack of Code Comments, and Incomplete Requirements.
  • Software Aging: Caused by "ignorant surgery," architectural erosion, and deadline pressure, leading to a high probability of system failure over time.
  • Evolution vs Maintenance: Maintenance focuses on modifying components without major architecture changes, while Evolution is a broader term encompassing major versions and redesigns.
Chapter 2: Key Issues in Software Maintenance
Core Concept: Technical and Management hurdles in long-term support.
  • Technical Issues:
    • Limited Understanding: A major hurdle when new developers join; they must undergo "Program Understanding" to avoid introducing new bugs.
    • Testing: Inadequate test plans and difficulties in reproducing bugs are primary risks.
    • Impact Analysis: The inability to predict the effect of changes, leading to unintended consequences.
  • Management & Costs:
    • Budgeting: Maintenance cost distribution typically follows the 60% Enhancement / 20% Adaptation / 20% Correction rule.
    • Estimation: COCOMO (Basic, Intermediate, Advanced) uses LOC to predict effort.
  • Quantitative Metrics:
    • Reliability: Measured by MTBF (operational hours / number of failures).
    • Efficiency: Measured by MTTR (average repair time).
    • OEE: Combines availability, performance, and quality.
Chapter 3: Evolution and Maintenance Models
Core Concept: Structured frameworks for managing the software lifecycle.
  • Reuse Oriented Models (Basili):
    • Quick Fix: Source code is modified first $\rightarrow$ documentation updated second. High risk.
    • Iterative Enhancement: Changes move from highest-level documents down to the code. Structured approach.
    • Full Reuse: Components of the old system are identified and modified to form a new system.
  • Staged Model (Rajlich & Bennett): Life spans are split into: Initial Development (high change, fresh knowledge) $\rightarrow$ Evolution (stable architecture, most lifespan spent here) $\rightarrow$ Servicing (minimal changes, knowledge loss) $\rightarrow$ Termination.
  • Legacy Management: Strategies include scrapping, continued maintenance, transforming through reengineering, or total replacement (which is risky due to undocumented business rules).
  • Refactoring vs Reengineering: Refactoring is a continuous preventivve process to stop degradation. Reengineering is a drastic restructuring of a legacy system to improve maintainability without changing functionality.

📌 Quick Summary: Maintenance Types

Type Goal Example
Corrective Fix bugs/faults Patching a security hole (e.g., Facebook token bug)
Adaptive Change Environment Updating tax calculations (GST ➔ SST)
Perfective Improve Quality/Perf Adding keyboard shortcuts, improving UI responsiveness
Preventive Avoid Future Issues Conducting regular code reviews, Automated testing

💡 Key Tips & Ideas

The Cost Rule: Remember that maintenance is usually 2x to 100x more expensive than development.
Technical Debt: Think of it as "financial debt"—short-term hacks create "interest" (entropy) that makes future changes harder.
Legacy Systems: They aren't just old code; they are "socio-technical" (code + outdated hardware + old business processes).
Refactoring vs Reeng: Refactoring is a "continuous cleaning" (preventive). Reengineering is a "major overhaul" of a legacy system.
